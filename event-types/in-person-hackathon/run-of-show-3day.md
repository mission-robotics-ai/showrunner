# Run of show — 3-day hackathon (Fri evening → Sun afternoon)

The schedule we ran, with the reasoning attached to each beat. Treat this as a skeleton: keep the beats and their logic, move the clock times to fit your venue and crowd. Scale reference: ~70–80 hackers, ~12 teams, real robot hardware on the floor.

## Day 0 (Thursday) — load-in

- **2:00pm — Partner load-in and testing opens.** Runs through the afternoon; Friday early afternoon is the overflow window.

*Why a full buffer day: hardware partners need to set up AND test before any hacker touches anything. Every robot that gets debugged Thursday is a team that starts building Friday instead of filing support tickets. If you host one hardware event, this is the beat you keep.*

## Day 1 (Friday) — doors, bootcamp, claim-and-build

- **~1:00pm — Final hardware drop lands.**
- **3:30pm — Doors open.**
- **4:00pm — Bootcamp begins, sharp.** Hands-on onboarding: the toolchain, data recording, the training loop, on real hardware.
- **5:30pm — Team formation + kickoff remarks.**
- **Evening — Hacking begins, runs into the night.**

*Why bootcamp before team formation, with a hook: we announced "bootcamp attendees get first pick of robots." That one sentence gets people in the door on time, gets the newest hackers skilled up before teams crystallize, and means teams form around people who've already touched the hardware.*

*Why Friday evening matters most: this is the claim-and-build window — teams pick robots and stake their approach. Every hardware partner should have someone on the floor tonight. A team that claims hardware and records first data Friday night starts Saturday building; a team that doesn't starts Saturday negotiating.*

## Day 2 (Saturday) — the build day

- **All day — building on the floor.**
- **Morning — the tooling crunch.** Setup and integration questions spike; partners with tools in play stay reachable.
- **2:00–3:00pm — Tech talks.**
- **Parallel — side competition** (ours: a robot mini-golf teleop tournament, most holes-in-one wins).
- **Night — training runs go overnight.**

*Why tech talks mid-afternoon, not morning: morning is the tooling crunch — pulling people off the floor then costs teams their momentum. The 2pm lull is real; talks fill it without stealing build time.*

*Why a side competition: it's a pressure valve for blocked teams, a spectacle for visitors, and — if it's a teleop game — it quietly generates demonstration data all weekend. Also: the finals broadcast becomes your judging-deliberation entertainment on Sunday (see judging.md).*

*Why "train overnight" is a design feature, not an accident: putting the pencils-down deadline on Sunday MORNING makes Saturday-night training runs the natural finish line. The event's rhythm does your deadline enforcement.*

## Day 3 (Sunday) — demos, judging, winners

- **10:00am — Pencils down. Live demos begin.** ~5 minutes per team, on real hardware.
- **~11:15–11:45am — Judges deliberate.** Side-competition finals broadcast keeps the room warm.
- **~2:00pm — Winners announced.**
- **3:00pm — Event ends; teardown.**

*Why demos at 10am and not later: everything must RUN at demo time — a morning deadline means overnight training finishes the work, and there's no dead half-day of teams "polishing" (read: breaking) working systems.*

*Why winners at 2pm with a gap after deliberation: the gap absorbs schedule slip, lets you stage the announcement properly (photos! get the photos!), and holds the room for the closing beat instead of letting it drain away team by team.*

## The three windows where partner floor-presence matters most

Tell your partners exactly this — it's the most useful paragraph in your partner email:
1. **Friday evening** — claim-and-build: hardware gets chosen and set up
2. **Saturday morning** — tooling crunch: integration questions spike
3. **Sunday morning** — demos: everything has to run; hardware owners present through judging

## Pre-mortem — the two capture failures to prevent on day 0

1. **Name a photo/video owner before doors open.** "Everyone will take photos" means nobody does. We have run an entire sold-out weekend and ended it with zero usable floor footage. Assign it like security or food — one name, one job.
2. **Wire your submission-form notifications somewhere you read.** If teams submit projects via a form, verify before the event that submission notifications actually reach an inbox your team monitors — and that judging results get written down in a sheet, same day, while everyone still remembers who won what.
